# Singapore HS Classification Guide

## Key Terms and Abbreviations

| Abbreviation | Full Term | Context |
|:------------|:----------|:--------|
| **HS** | Harmonized System | International product classification system |
| **AHTN** | ASEAN Harmonized Tariff Nomenclature | 8-digit ASEAN extension of HS |
| **GIR** | General Interpretive Rules | 6 rules for classifying goods |
| **EN** | Explanatory Notes | WCO's official classification guidance |
| **WCO** | World Customs Organization | Maintains the HS system |

## What is the Harmonised System (HS)?

The Harmonised System is an international nomenclature (at 6-digit level) developed by the World Customs Organisation (WCO) for categorizing traded goods globally. It provides a standardized system for classifying merchandise in international trade.

## Structure of HS Codes

### Hierarchical Organization

The system organizes merchandise through:

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Sections | 21 broad categories |
| Chapters | 97 product groupings |
| Headings | 4-digit codes |
| Subheadings | 6-digit codes (international standard) |

### Supporting Elements
- Section and Chapter Notes with subheading notes
- Systematically ordered headings divided into subheadings
- General Interpretative Rules (GIR)

### Arrangement Principle
Products are arranged in order of their degree of manufacture, both across sections and within individual chapters (from raw materials to finished goods).

## HS Code Digit Structure

| Digits | Level | Example |
|--------|-------|---------|
| 2 digits | Chapter | 87 - Vehicles |
| 4 digits | Heading | 8703 - Motor vehicles for transport of persons |
| 6 digits | Subheading (International) | 8703.21 - Spark ignition, ≤1000cc |
| 8 digits | AHTN (ASEAN) | 8703.21.11 - CBU, new |
| 10 digits | National (Singapore) | 8703.21.11.00 - Specific national use |

## ASEAN Harmonised Tariff Nomenclature (AHTN)

The ASEAN Harmonised Tariff Nomenclature extends HS to 8 digits, ensuring consistent classification across member states.

**Current version**: AHTN 2022, incorporating all WCO amendments.

This ensures uniform product classification for:
- ASEAN Free Trade Area (AFTA)
- Preferential tariff application
- Trade statistics

## Classification Process

The standard approach involves sequentially determining the correct classification level:

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Determine 4-digit heading** (primary category)
2. **Identify 1-dash subheading** (first refinement)
3. **Narrow to 2-dash subheading** (further specificity)
4. **Continue to lower levels** as needed

**Critical Rule**: Do not proceed directly to the lower-level subheadings without first determining the appropriate heading.

## How to Apply for a Classification Ruling

**What is a Classification Ruling?**
A formal decision by Singapore Customs on the correct HS code for your product. Rulings provide certainty and are binding.

**When to Request a Ruling**:
- Product classification is unclear
- Product is new or innovative
- You want advance certainty before import/export
- Previous classifications were challenged

### Step-by-Step Application Process

**Step 1: Prepare Documentation**
| Required | Optional (Helpful) |
|----------|-------------------|
| Completed application form | Product photos |
| Product samples or detailed specs | Manufacturing process description |
| Technical brochures | Competitor classification references |
| Material composition | Intended use statement |

**Step 2: Submit via Customs@SG Portal**
1. Log in to customs.gov.sg
2. Navigate to "Harmonised System Classification" section
3. Select "Apply for Classification Ruling"
4. Upload documents and complete form
5. Pay application fee: **S$75** (non-refundable)

**Step 3: Processing**
- Standard processing time: **30 working days** from complete submission
- Complex cases may take longer
- Customs may request additional information

**Step 4: Receive Ruling**
- Ruling issued in writing
- Specifies the HS code and reasoning
- Validity: As stated in ruling letter (typically 3 years)
- Ruling is binding unless product changes

## General Interpretative Rules (GIR)

The GIR provide structured guidance for uniform classification interpretation and must be applied in sequential order.

### The Six Rules

| Rule | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Rule 1 | Classification by terms of headings and section/chapter notes |
| Rule 2 | (a) Incomplete/unfinished goods; (b) Mixtures and combinations |
| Rule 3 | (a) Most specific heading; (b) Essential character; (c) Last in numerical order |
| Rule 4 | Most akin goods |
| Rule 5 | (a) Cases and containers; (b) Packing materials |
| Rule 6 | Classification at subheading level |

## Requesting a Classification Ruling

For complex or uncertain classifications, apply for an official ruling from Singapore Customs.

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Fee | S$75 per product |

### When to Request a Ruling

- Products with multiple possible classifications
- High-value imports where duty rates vary significantly

## General Interpretative Rules (GIR) — Detailed Guide

The GIR is a set of 6 rules for classification of goods, ensuring uniform legal interpretation of the Harmonized System Nomenclature. These rules must be applied in sequential order.

### Rule 1 — Headings and Notes
Classification is determined according to the terms of the headings and any relative Section or Chapter Notes. Section/Chapter titles are for ease of reference only.

**Examples**:
- Section XV is entitled "Base metals and articles of base metal" but jewellery of base metal is classified in Section XIV.
- Live horses are classified in Heading 01.01.

### Rule 2 — Incomplete/Unfinished Goods and Mixtures
**(a)** References to an article include that article incomplete or unfinished, provided it has the essential character of the complete article. Also includes articles presented unassembled or disassembled.

**Examples**: A car without wheels is considered a complete car. A complete set of wooden panels for assembly into a cupboard is considered a finished cupboard.

**(b)** References to a material or substance include mixtures or combinations with other materials. Classification of goods with more than one material follows Rule 3.

**Examples**: Milk to which vitamins or minerals have been added. A pack of cornflakes with nuts and raisins.

### Rule 3 — Multiple Possible Headings
When goods are classifiable under two or more headings:

**(a)** The heading providing the most specific description is preferred over a more general heading.

**(b)** Composite goods, mixtures, and goods in sets are classified by the material or component giving them their essential character.

**(c)** When (a) and (b) cannot determine classification, classify under the last heading in numerical order among those equally meriting consideration.

### Rule 4 — Most Akin
Goods which cannot be classified under Rules 1-3 are classified under the heading appropriate to the goods to which they are most akin (similar).

### Rule 5 — Cases and Packing
**(a)** Camera cases, musical instrument cases, and similar containers specially shaped for specific articles are classified with those articles when normally sold together.

**(b)** Packing materials and containers presented with goods are classified with those goods, unless they are clearly suitable for repetitive use.

### Rule 6 — Subheading Classification
Classification at subheading level follows the same principles as headings, with subheading notes and rules applied at the appropriate level.
